01 — About

Background & Interests

I am a Chilean sociologist and PhD candidate in Criminology at the University of Lausanne, where my research sits at the intersection of the sociology of religion and criminology. I have received academic training in Chile, France, the United States, and Switzerland — a trajectory that has deeply shaped my comparative and international perspective.

My work combines quantitative methods, spatial analysis, and comparative designs to study lethal violence, religiosity, and social inequality. I am particularly interested in subnational variation in homicide rates, the territorial concentration of violence, and macro-social factors — including religion and culture — associated with violent outcomes.

02 — Research

Areas of Study

🌎

Homicide in Latin America

Regional and subnational variation in homicide, with attention to long-term trends, concentration, and internal heterogeneity. I examine why some countries and subregions sustain persistently high rates while others diverge.

🗺️

Homicide in Chile

Municipal-level and spatial approaches to lethal violence in Chile — including the territorial concentration of homicide, local predictors of violence, and the spatial clustering of high-risk areas across time.

✝️

Religion & Violence

The relationship between religiosity, culture, and violent outcomes. I investigate contextual effects of religious environments — including secularization dynamics and evangelical growth — on violence and moral norms.

📊

Quantitative & Spatial Methods

Multilevel models, logistic and count models, spatial autocorrelation, comparative designs, and data visualization. Methods are always at the service of substantive sociological questions.
